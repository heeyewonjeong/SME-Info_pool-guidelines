Camera Angle Types

Reference guide for classifying camera angle in news and movie video content. If a single segment shows more than one angle over time, tag it as More Than One and list every angle in chronological order.

Camera Angle Types — 6 Categories
01 Eye-Level 02 High Angle 03 Low Angle 04 Dutch Angle 05 Overhead 06 Aerial 07 More Than One
Camera Angle 01
Eye-Level
Neutral

The camera is positioned at the approximate eye height of the subject, presenting a neutral, natural perspective that mirrors how a person would view the scene in real life.

Position
At the subject's eye height
Effect
Neutral, natural, relatable perspective
Stability
Fixed or handheld — either is common
Framing
Camera and subject roughly level
  • Camera height matches the subject's eyes — neither looking up nor down
  • Most common angle in interviews, dialogue scenes, and studio anchor shots
  • Creates a sense of equality and directness between subject and viewer
  • Default angle when no deliberate high or low choice is evident
Rule → If the camera is roughly level with the subject's eyes with no clear upward or downward tilt, classify as Eye-Level.
News Interview Anchor Desk Dialogue Scene Documentary
Camera Angle 02
High Angle
Looking Down

The camera is positioned above the subject, looking down. This makes the subject appear smaller, weaker, or more vulnerable relative to the viewer.

Position
Above the subject, angled downward
Effect
Subject appears smaller, weaker, or vulnerable
Stability
Fixed, crane, or handheld
Framing
Looking down onto the subject, not straight down
  • Camera is placed higher than the subject and tilts downward toward them
  • Often used to convey vulnerability, isolation, or diminished power
  • Common in news footage of crowds, protests, or scene overviews
  • Subject is still seen at an angle — not directly from above
Rule → If the camera looks down at the subject from above but not directly overhead, classify as High Angle.

What this angle is NOT

  • Not Overhead — the camera does not look straight down at a near-90° angle
  • Not Aerial — the shot is scene-specific, not a wide establishing view from great height

Camera Angle 03
Low Angle
Looking Up

The camera is positioned below the subject, looking up. This makes the subject appear larger, more powerful, or dominant.

Position
Below the subject, angled upward
Effect
Subject appears larger, powerful, dominant
Stability
Fixed or handheld
Framing
Looking up at the subject
  • Camera is placed lower than the subject and tilts upward toward them
  • Often used to convey authority, power, or intimidation
  • Common in political speeches, courtroom drama, or hero-framing shots
  • The subject dominates the frame relative to the background
Rule → If the camera is positioned below the subject and tilts upward, making the subject appear dominant, classify as Low Angle.
Political Speech Courtroom Drama Hero Framing
Camera Angle 04
Dutch Angle
Tilted

The camera is deliberately tilted off the horizontal axis, so the horizon line appears slanted rather than level.

Position
Camera roll axis tilted, not level with horizon
Effect
Tension, disorientation, unease
Stability
Fixed tilt — intentional, not accidental shake
Framing
Horizon line appears diagonal in frame
  • Horizontal lines in the frame (horizon, buildings, tables) appear slanted rather than level
  • Used deliberately to signal tension, instability, or psychological unease
  • Common in thriller, action, or breaking-news chaos coverage
  • Distinguish from camera shake — a Dutch Angle is a static tilt, not jitter
Rule → If the horizon or other horizontal reference lines are clearly slanted rather than level, classify as Dutch Angle.
Thriller Action Scene Breaking News Chaos
Camera Angle 05
Overhead
Top-Down

The camera is positioned directly above the subject, looking straight down — a close-range, top-down view within the scene, not a wide establishing shot from far above.

Position
Directly above, near-vertical downward angle
Effect
Graphic, abstract, top-down perspective
Stability
Fixed rig or mounted camera
Framing
Subject seen from directly above, near 90° downward
  • Camera looks almost straight down, close to a 90-degree angle
  • Flattens depth — often used for tabletop shots, choreography, or graphic compositions
  • Close-range and scene-specific, unlike a wide establishing view
Rule → If the camera looks nearly straight down at the subject from a relatively close range, classify as Overhead.

What this angle is NOT

  • Not High Angle — the camera looks straight down, not at a partial downward angle
  • Not Aerial — the shot is close-range and scene-specific, not a wide environmental establishing view
Tabletop Shot Choreography Graphic Composition
Camera Angle 06
Aerial
Establishing

A wide shot captured from significant height — via drone, helicopter, or crane — showing a broad view of the location or environment, not a specific subject up close.

Position
Significant height above the scene (drone, helicopter, crane)
Effect
Scale, geography, establishing context
Stability
Typically stabilized (drone / gimbal)
Framing
Wide view of environment; subjects appear small or absent
  • Captured from significant height, well above the immediate scene
  • Used to establish location, scale, or geography — stadiums, cityscapes, disaster areas
  • Subjects, if visible, appear small within a much larger environment
  • Common in news scene-setting shots and movie establishing shots
Rule → If the shot is from significant height and shows the broader environment rather than a close view of a subject, classify as Aerial.

Tagging Rule — Multi-Angle Segments
Special Case
More Than One
Sequenced Tagging

Some segments do not hold a single angle for their full duration — the camera may physically move between angles (e.g. craning down from High Angle to Eye-Level), or the segment may contain an internal cut between two distinct angles. In these cases, do not force one tag onto the whole segment.

  • Select More Than One as the angle tag for the segment
  • List every angle that appears in the segment, in chronological order, using the format Angle 1 → Angle 2 → Angle 3
  • If the same angle reappears later in the segment after switching away, list it again in its correct chronological position
  • If the segment holds a single angle for its entire duration — even with minor handheld movement — tag that single angle only, not More Than One
Rule → If the angle changes within a single segment — whether through camera movement or an internal cut — tag More Than One and list every angle in the order it appears, separated by →.

Example Segments

Camera starts overhead on a crowd, then cranes down to eye-level as a reporter walks in.
More Than One — Overhead → Eye-Level
Interview begins eye-level; the camera tilts into a Dutch angle during a dramatic reveal.
More Than One — Eye-Level → Dutch Angle
Aerial establishing shot of a stadium cuts to a low-angle hero shot of a player.
More Than One — Aerial → Low Angle
Static low-angle shot throughout, with only minor handheld movement and no cut.
Low Angle (single angle — not More Than One)
