Shot Type
Classification

Multi-tag annotation system for consistent, searchable shot type classification across news and movie video content. Apply these labels when annotating broadcast footage, interviews, and narrative scenes.

System Overview
Tagging Structure

Every shot gets two tags

Each shot must be tagged with exactly one tag from each category. Both tags are required — never leave one blank.

Tag 1 Framing

How the camera frames the subject — defined by where the frame cuts off on the body, or how wide the view is.

Extreme Close-Up Close-Up Medium Full Master Moving Master POV Insert
Tag 2 Composition

How many subjects appear in the frame and what spatial relationship they have to each other and the camera.

Single Dirty Single OTS Two-Shot Three-Shot Group N/A
When to use Composition: N/A

Use N/A for the Composition tag when the shot contains no human subjects (Insert shots, object close-ups, scenery) or when the framing tag is POV. For Master and Moving Master shots where subjects are incidental to the environment, N/A is also acceptable.

Decision Process
1

Is the shot a close-up of an object or story-relevant detail (not a person)?
→ Tag 1: Insert | Tag 2: N/A — stop here.

2

Is it a wide shot with active camera movement (pan, track, dolly) maintaining a wide view throughout?
→ Tag 1: Moving Master | Tag 2: determine composition below.

3

Determine Tag 1 (Framing) — where does the frame cut off?
Only a fragment visible (eyes, hands) → Extreme Close-Up
Chest and up → Close-Up
Waist and up → Medium
Head to toe, subject is focal point → Full
Wide, environment equally prominent → Master
Subject's POV perspective → POV

4

Determine Tag 2 (Composition) — how many subjects, and how are they arranged?
One subject, clean frame → Single
One subject dominant, another partially at frame edge → Dirty Single
Camera over one person's shoulder looking at another → OTS
Exactly 2 subjects, roughly equal weight → Two-Shot
Exactly 3 subjects, roughly equal weight → Three-Shot
4 or more subjects, roughly equal weight → Group
No human subjects in frame → N/A

Tag 1 — Framing
Tag 1

Framing

Select the one tag that best describes where the camera cuts off the primary subject.

ECU
Reference image
ECU
Extreme Close-Up
Fragment of subject only

A shot isolating a very specific detail — eyes, mouth, hands, feet — where the full subject is not visible.

  • Only a fragment of the subject fills the frame
  • No broader context is visible
  • Used for dramatic emphasis or critical detail
Rule

If you cannot identify the full subject and only one specific body part or detail fills the frame, it's an ECU.

CU
Reference image
CU
Close-Up
Chest and up framing

A shot framing the subject from approximately the chest up, centering attention on facial expression or a specific object.

  • Background is largely absent or out of focus
  • Facial expressions dominate for people; a specific detail fills the frame for objects
  • A partially visible secondary figure at the edge doesn't change the classification
  • In news, draws attention to facial expressions in interviews or critical field details
Rule

If the primary subject is framed from the chest up or closer and clearly dominates the frame, it's a Close-Up. Do not reclassify as Two-Shot unless the secondary subject occupies equal visual weight.

MED
Reference image
Medium
Medium
Waist-up framing

A shot framing one or more subjects from approximately the waist up, balancing subject detail with some environmental context.

  • Frame cuts at the waist or mid-torso
  • Upper body and facial expressions are clearly readable
  • Applies regardless of subject count — use Tag 2 to specify how many
  • In news, commonly includes reporter-interviewee scenes and anchor shots
Rule

If subjects are visible from the waist up and facial expressions are readable, it's a Medium. Use the Composition tag to specify how many subjects appear.

FULL
Reference image
Full
Full
Head-to-toe framing

A shot framing the subject from head to toe, with the subject's complete figure as the focal point.

  • The complete body is visible
  • Background provides spatial context but does not compete with the subject as focal point
  • Common when capturing full-body action or movement — walking, gesturing, exiting a vehicle
  • If the feet or head are cut off, reclassify as Medium
Rule

If one or more subjects are visible head to toe and their posture or movement is the clear focal point, it's a Full. If the environment becomes equally prominent, consider Master instead.

MSTR
Reference image
Master
Master
Wide, scene-establishing

A wide shot establishing the entire scene — environment, atmosphere, and all subjects in spatial relation to each other.

  • Environment and setting are as prominent as any individual subject
  • Individual subjects do not need to be fully in frame
  • Establishes overall space rather than highlighting one person's action
  • Includes studio-wide views where an anchor faces a large monitor (single undivided camera)
Rule

Ask — "Is this shot showing a person's action, or showing the space they're in?" If the space is the point, it's a Master. If a subject's full-body action is the clear focal point, it's a Full.

MOV MSTR
Reference image
Moving Master
Moving Master
Wide shot, active camera movement

A master shot where the camera actively moves (pan, track, dolly) while maintaining a wide view of the scene throughout.

  • Wide scene-establishing framing is maintained throughout the movement
  • Common when a reporter or subject walks through a location as the camera follows alongside
  • If framing shifts to isolate a single subject, end the segment and start a new one
Rule

Wide view + active camera movement following the action = Moving Master. If it stops being wide, end the segment.

POV
Reference image
POV
POV
Subject's visual perspective

A shot that shows what a character is seeing from their perspective, as if the camera is their eyes.

  • Camera mimics what a character sees through their eyes
  • The shot moves and orients as if following a character's gaze
  • Always paired with Composition: N/A
Rule

If the shot moves as if it's someone's gaze or directly represents a character's visual perspective, it's a POV. Use Composition: N/A.

INS
Reference image
Insert
Insert
Object or detail close-up

A close-up shot of a story-relevant object or detail — a letter, phone screen, document, evidence item.

  • Isolates and emphasizes a specific object or text detail with narrative or informational importance
  • No human subject is the focal point
  • Always paired with Composition: N/A
Rule

If the shot isolates a specific object or text detail important to the story with no human focal point, it's an Insert. Use Composition: N/A.

Tag 2 — Composition
Tag 2

Composition

Select the one tag that best describes how many subjects appear and their spatial relationship in the frame.

SNGL
Reference image
Single
Single
One subject, clean frame

One subject clearly dominates the frame with no other subjects partially or fully visible.

  • One person is the clear subject of the shot
  • No secondary figures visible at the frame edge
  • Most common composition in news interviews and field reporting
Rule

If only one person is in the frame, it's a Single.

DIRTY
Reference image
Dirty Single
Dirty Single
Partial second subject at edge

One subject clearly dominates, with a partial view of another person at the frame edge — typically a shoulder, arm, or back of a head.

  • Secondary figure never fully present and doesn't draw equal attention — often out of focus or at the very edge
  • Common in field interviews (reporter's shoulder) or press conferences (adjacent speaker overlapping edge)
  • If the secondary subject grows to occupy equal visual weight, reclassify as Two-Shot
Rule

If the frame is clearly focused on one subject and another person partially intrudes at the edge without drawing equal attention, it's a Dirty Single. It is NOT a Two-Shot unless the secondary figure reaches equal prominence.

OTS
Reference image
OTS
OTS (Over-The-Shoulder)
Foreground shoulder, focus on another

Camera positioned behind one person's shoulder, looking toward another subject.

  • One subject's shoulder and/or back occupies the foreground; a second subject is the visual focus
  • The foreground shoulder is intentional — it establishes spatial relationship between the two subjects
  • Common in conversation scenes and interview setups
Rule

If you can see part of someone's shoulder or back in the foreground while the camera focuses on another subject, and this framing feels intentional, it's an OTS — not a Dirty Single or Two-Shot.

2-SHOT
Reference image
Two-Shot
Two-Shot
Two subjects, equal weight

Exactly two subjects are framed together with roughly equal visual weight.

  • Both subjects are clearly visible and neither dominates
  • Both must be in the same physical space, captured by one undivided camera
  • Framing distance (Tag 1) is separate — focus only on equal prominence here
Rule

If exactly two subjects share the frame with roughly equal prominence, it's a Two-Shot regardless of how much of their bodies are visible. If one subject clearly dominates, use Single or Dirty Single instead.

3-SHOT
Reference image
Three-Shot
Three-Shot
Three subjects, equal weight

Exactly three subjects are framed together with roughly equal visual weight.

  • All three subjects are clearly visible and roughly balanced
  • Same equal-prominence rule as Two-Shot
  • If one subject clearly dominates the other two, reclassify using the dominant subject's composition
Rule

If exactly three subjects share the frame with roughly equal prominence, it's a Three-Shot.

GROUP
Reference image
Group
Group
Four or more subjects

Four or more subjects are framed together with roughly equal visual weight.

  • Four or more subjects clearly visible, none dominant over the others
  • All in the same physical space, captured by one undivided camera
  • If the environment becomes dominant, consider Master for Framing instead
Rule

If four or more subjects share the frame with roughly equal prominence, it's a Group. If the shot feels more like an establishing wide shot of a location than a portrait of a group, use Master for Framing.

N/A
Reference image
N/A
N/A
No human subjects to classify

No human subjects are present, or the framing tag makes a composition tag irrelevant.

  • Use when the shot is an Insert (object/detail only)
  • Use when the framing tag is POV
  • Use for pure scenery or environment with no identifiable human subjects
  • Use when framing is Master/Moving Master and subjects are entirely incidental
Rule

If there are no human subjects to classify by their arrangement, use N/A.

Edge Cases — Common Confusion Points

Full vs. Master

Ask: "Is this shot showing what a person is doing, or showing the space they're in?" If a subject's full-body movement or posture is the clear reason for the shot, it's Full. If the environment, setting, or spatial relationships are equally important as any individual subject, it's Master. When the subject's feet are cut off, it cannot be Full regardless of how prominent they appear.

Dirty Single vs. Two-Shot vs. OTS

The test is equal visual weight. In a Dirty Single, one subject clearly dominates and the other is a partial intrusion at the edge — often blurred. In a Two-Shot, both subjects command roughly equal attention. In an OTS, one subject's shoulder is intentionally in the foreground as a compositional device — the framing is deliberately asymmetric to establish spatial context. When in doubt: if you'd crop out the secondary figure without losing the shot's meaning, it's a Dirty Single.

Master + Group vs. Full + Group

If four or more people are visible with their full bodies and the environment is equally prominent (e.g. a wide press conference room shot), use Master + Group. If four or more people are visible with full bodies but the group's collective action or presence — not the space — is the focal point, use Full + Group.

Moving Master — when to end the segment

If the camera movement causes framing to shift from a wide view to isolating a single subject (e.g. it zooms in mid-shot), end the Moving Master segment at the transition point and start a new segment with the appropriate framing tag. Do not classify the entire shot as Moving Master if part of it is a medium or close-up.
