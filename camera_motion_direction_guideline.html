Camera Motion,
Movement & Direction

Reference guide for segmenting video into camera-motion timestamps and tagging each segment with a camera_motion and camera_direction label.

Part 1 — Creating the Timestamps
Step 0

What a "segment" is

Before tagging camera_motion or camera_direction, you must first cut the video into segments purely based on camera motion. This is a separate, prior task from labeling shot type, content, or subject — ignore everything except how the camera itself is moving.

1
Watch the full clip once at normal speed
Before making any cuts, play the video through one time without pausing. This gives you a rough map of where the camera changes behavior (e.g. "it pans, then holds still, then zooms in"). Do not create timestamps on this first pass — just build a mental outline.
2
Re-watch frame-by-frame around every suspected change
Step through the video frame by frame (not just scrubbing) near each point where you think the camera's behavior changes. Camera motion changes are often only a few frames long — scrubbing at normal or fast speed will cause you to miss the exact frame the change starts or ends on.
Why this matters

Your timestamp boundaries must land on the exact frame where the motion or direction changes — not the frame before, not the frame after. A boundary that is off by even 2–3 frames is considered incorrect.

3
Start a new segment whenever motion OR direction changes
A single continuous segment must have one consistent camera_motion value and one consistent camera_direction value from its start frame to its end frame. The moment either value would change, close the current segment and open a new one — even if the change seems minor.
  • Motion changes (e.g. pan → static): new segment.
  • Direction changes with the same motion type (e.g. truck_left → truck_right): new segment.
  • Motion and direction both change at once (e.g. dolly_in/push → truck_left/left): new segment.
  • A hold between two moves is its own static segment only if it lasts 0.5 seconds or longer. If the hold is shorter than 0.5 seconds (e.g. a 3–5 frame pause), it is not perceptible on its own — do not create a separate static segment for it. Instead, treat it as one direct transition between the two moves (e.g. a left move cutting straight into a right move) and place the single segment boundary at the frame where the direction visibly changes.
Part 2 — Tagging Each Segment: camera_motion
Tag 1

camera_motion

Describes how the camera is moving (or not moving) during the segment: which physical or mechanical action produces the motion. Choose exactly one value per segment.

static
Static
No camera movement

The camera is fixed on a support — a tripod, mounted rig, or any locked-off mount — and does not move or rotate at all. Anything moving in the frame is the subject, not the camera.

  • Locked-off tripod shots, fixed security-style cameras
  • Frame composition does not shift, rotate, or change scale
  • Any shot where the camera is held in the operator's hands (even if they are trying to hold it very still) is handheld, not static — a mounted support is required for static, see below
Rule

Tag static only if the camera is on a fixed mount/support and its position, angle, and scale are identical from the first frame to the last frame of the segment. If the camera is held in hands rather than mounted, tag handheld instead, even if it looks steady.

pan
Pan
Horizontal rotation on a fixed axis

The camera body stays in one fixed position but rotates left or right around its own vertical axis, like a head turning. The lens sweeps across the scene horizontally.

  • Camera position does not translate through space, only rotates
  • Used to follow horizontal action or reveal a wider environment
  • Speed is smooth and controlled — a very fast sweeping pan used as a transition is whip_pan instead
Rule

If the camera rotates horizontally in place (no translation) at a normal, controlled speed, tag pan.

tilt
Tilt
Vertical rotation on a fixed axis

The camera body stays in one fixed position but rotates up or down around its own horizontal axis, the vertical equivalent of a pan.

  • Camera position does not translate through space, only rotates
  • Common for revealing height (e.g. tilting up a building) or following vertical action
Rule

If the camera rotates vertically in place (no translation), tag tilt.

dolly_in
Dolly In
Camera moves toward subject

The physical camera rig itself travels forward through space, getting closer to the subject. Perspective and parallax shift naturally because the camera is actually relocating — unlike a zoom, which only changes lens focal length.

  • Camera is physically on a dolly, slider, drone, gimbal, or being carried forward
  • Background elements shift relative to each other as the camera moves (parallax)
Rule

If the camera rig physically translates closer to the subject, tag dolly_in. If only the lens changes focal length, use zoom_in instead.

dolly_out
Dolly Out
Camera moves away from subject

The physical camera rig travels backward through space, away from the subject, revealing more of the surrounding environment as it retreats.

  • Camera physically translates away — not just a lens zoom-out
  • Often used to reveal scale or context at the end of a shot
Rule

If the camera rig physically translates away from the subject, tag dolly_out. If only the lens focal length changes, use zoom_out instead.

truck_left
Truck Left
Camera moves laterally left

The entire camera rig translates sideways to the left while keeping roughly the same distance and framing on the subject — think of a camera sliding along a rail parallel to the action.

  • Camera moves left-to-right in space, not rotating in place like a pan
  • Distance to the subject stays roughly constant
Rule

If the camera rig physically slides sideways to the left, tag truck_left. If the camera only rotates in place, use pan instead.

truck_right
Truck Right
Camera moves laterally right

The entire camera rig translates sideways to the right while keeping roughly the same distance and framing on the subject.

  • Camera moves right in space, not rotating in place like a pan
  • Distance to the subject stays roughly constant
Rule

If the camera rig physically slides sideways to the right, tag truck_right. If the camera only rotates in place, use pan instead.

pedestal_down
Pedestal Down
Camera moves vertically downward

The entire camera rig moves straight down vertically while keeping the same framing angle — it does not tilt, it translates downward in space. Think of a wheeled studio pedestal dolly: the camera sits on top of a vertical column, and that column lowers straight down, like a barber chair's hydraulic lift going down. This is not a shoulder-mounted or handheld shot — a handheld camera being lowered by hand is handheld, not pedestal_down.

  • Vertical translation of the whole rig, not a rotation of the lens angle
  • Distinct from crane_down, which involves an arm swinging on a pivot
Rule

If the framing angle stays constant and the path is a straight vertical line, tag pedestal_down. See the disambiguation section below for the exact visual test — and when in doubt, this is the default choice.

crane_down
Crane Down
Crane arm moves downward

The camera is mounted on the end of a crane or jib arm — a rigid arm that pivots on a fulcrum, like a seesaw or a fishing rod. As the near end of the arm is lowered by the operator, the far end (with the camera) travels down, often through a curved arc. The framing angle frequently drifts as the arm pivots, but a skilled operator can also counter-tilt the camera head to keep the angle level throughout — in that case a crane move can look almost identical to a straight pedestal_down. This is not someone climbing up or down a ladder — it's the arm itself pivoting, distinct from a straight vertical pedestal move.

  • Camera height decreases along a curved or arm-driven path
  • Framing angle often (not always) drifts gradually as the arm pivots — a level, counter-tilted crane move is still crane_down
Rule

Tag crane_down when you can see the arm/boom itself, a clearly curved path, or a changing framing angle. See the disambiguation section below — note that a well-operated crane can be visually indistinguishable from pedestal_down, so treat the visual test as a strong signal, not an absolute rule.

handheld
Handheld
Handheld, unstabilized movement

The camera is held in the operator's hands rather than mounted on a tripod, dolly, or rig — it is the equipment setup, not just the visible shake, that defines this tag. Handheld shots typically show small, irregular shakes, bobs, or drifts, but any shot held by hand belongs in this category, even if the operator is holding it very steady or using a handheld stabilizer.

  • Visible micro-jitter or organic sway is the most common giveaway, but is not required — a very steady handheld shot is still handheld, not static
  • Can co-occur with an intentional move (e.g. a handheld truck) — tag handheld whenever the camera is hand-held, even while also panning/trucking/dollying
Rule

If the camera is being held in hands (not on a mount), tag handheld — regardless of how steady it looks. Reserve static strictly for camera that is on a fixed mount/support.

whip_pan
Whip Pan
Very fast pan used as a transition device

An extremely fast horizontal pan that blurs the image heavily, typically used as a stylistic transition between two shots or moments rather than to smoothly follow action.

  • Motion blur across most/all of the frame is a strong indicator
  • Usually very short in duration (often under half a second)
Rule

If a horizontal pan is fast enough to heavily blur the frame and reads as a transition, tag whip_pan rather than pan.

zoom_in
Zoom In
Lens zoom toward subject

The physical camera position stays fixed in space; only the lens focal length increases, magnifying the subject. Because the camera itself does not move, there is little to no parallax shift between foreground and background.

  • Camera rig does not translate; only field of view narrows
  • Background compresses/flattens rather than shifting via parallax
Rule

If the camera position is fixed and only the framing narrows via the lens, tag zoom_in. If the rig itself physically travels forward, use dolly_in.

zoom_out
Zoom Out
Lens zoom away from subject

The physical camera position stays fixed in space; only the lens focal length decreases, widening the field of view and making the subject appear smaller.

  • Camera rig does not translate; only field of view widens
  • No parallax shift between foreground and background layers
Rule

If the camera position is fixed and only the framing widens via the lens, tag zoom_out. If the rig itself physically travels backward, use dolly_out.

arc
Arc / Orbit
Camera travels in a curved path around the subject

The camera physically moves along a curved or circular path around the subject while continuously re-aiming to keep the subject centered — effectively trucking and panning (or trucking and tilting) at the same time, so that the subject stays in frame as the camera's position sweeps around it.

  • Camera position changes along a curve, not a straight line like truck_left/truck_right
  • The framing continuously re-centers on the subject, unlike a pure pan where the camera doesn't relocate
  • Background behind the subject appears to rotate/reveal a different side as the camera orbits
Rule

If the camera physically travels around the subject in a curved path while re-aiming to keep it centered, tag arc — do not force this into truck_left/truck_right or pan.

other
Other
Does not fit above

Use only when the camera motion clearly does not match any of the enum values above — for example, an unusual rig move (roll/Dutch spin, complex compound rig motion) or motion that cannot be confidently classified.

  • Do not use other just because you are unsure — first re-watch the clip frame-by-frame and re-check the definitions above
  • If you use other repeatedly for the same recognizable motion, flag it to your lead so it can be reviewed for a possible new enum value
Critical Disambiguation — Dolly vs. Zoom

This is the most common annotator confusion. Both make the subject appear larger or smaller, but the physical cause is completely different.

dolly_in / dolly_out
  • Camera rig physically travels through space
  • Background objects shift position relative to each other (parallax)
  • Perspective/depth relationships change naturally
zoom_in / zoom_out
  • Camera rig stays in one fixed spot
  • Background objects do not shift relative to each other, only scale together
  • Image can look slightly "flattened" or "compressed" at long focal lengths
Quick test

Watch two background objects at different depths. If they slide relative to one another as the shot progresses, the camera physically moved (dolly). If they stay in the same relative position and only scale up/down together, it is a lens change (zoom).

Critical Disambiguation — Pan vs. Truck

Both can look similar on screen (the frame sweeps sideways), but the camera's physical behavior is different.

pan
  • Camera stays in one fixed spot and rotates on its axis
  • Nearby and distant objects sweep across frame at the same rate (no parallax)
truck_left / truck_right
  • Camera physically slides sideways through space
  • Nearby objects move across frame faster than distant objects (parallax)
Watch out for arc/orbit shots

If the camera moves sideways and continuously re-aims to keep the subject centered as it curves around them, that's an arc/orbit shot — a combination of trucking and panning at once, not a pure pan or pure truck_left/truck_right. Tag these as arc instead — see its own card above.

Critical Disambiguation — Pedestal Down vs. Crane Down

These two are genuinely hard to tell apart by eye, since both simply move the frame downward. Don't try to guess based on how "big" or "cinematic" the move looks — use the two concrete visual checks below instead.

In plain terms: pedestal_down is a vertical column sinking straight down, like a barber chair's hydraulic lift. crane_down is a rigid arm pivoting on a fulcrum, like a seesaw or fishing rod, swinging the camera down through a curve. Neither one is a person climbing a ladder, and neither one is a shoulder-mounted camera — that would be handheld.

pedestal_down
  • The camera's angle stays parallel/constant — it does not tip forward or backward as it descends
  • The path is a straight vertical line, like an elevator dropping
  • Background elements keep the same relative alignment to each other; only their position in frame shifts upward as the camera sinks
crane_down
  • The camera's angle often gradually changes as it descends (tips down and/or drifts forward/back), because it is riding the end of a pivoting arm — but a skilled operator can counter-tilt to keep the angle level, so this cue is not guaranteed
  • The physical path curves, even if the framing angle is kept level by the operator
  • When the angle does drift, the horizon line or background perspective visibly tilts/shifts relative to the frame edges, not just moves straight up
Quick test

Watch the horizon line or a straight background edge (a wall, a doorframe, a table edge). If it stays perfectly parallel to the top/bottom of the frame the whole time, treat it as pedestal_down unless you can also see the crane arm/rig itself. If it visibly rotates or tilts relative to the frame as the camera drops, it's crane_down.

If you genuinely can't tell

A level, counter-tilted crane move can look visually identical to a pedestal move — this is expected, not a sign you're doing something wrong. When there's no visible rig and the angle stays level throughout, default to pedestal_down. Reserve crane_down for segments where you can see the arm/rig itself, a clearly curved path, or a changing framing angle.

Critical Disambiguation — Tilt (Down) vs. Crane/Pedestal Down

All three point the frame downward, but the question is whether the camera's physical location changes, or only its orientation.

tilt (direction: down)
  • The camera stays at the exact same physical location/height — only its orientation rotates downward, like a person nodding their head while standing still
  • Background elements do not shift relative to each other (no parallax); the same vantage point is just aimed lower
crane_down / pedestal_down
  • The camera's actual height/location changes — like a person going from standing to crouching, their eye-level itself moves
  • Background elements shift relative to each other as the vantage point relocates (parallax)
Quick test

Ask: did the camera's vantage point (its height and position in the room/scene) actually change, or did it just look somewhere else from the same spot? Same spot, new angle → tilt. Different vantage point → crane_down (arcing) or pedestal_down (straight vertical).

Part 3 — Tagging Each Segment: camera_direction
Tag 2

camera_direction

Describes the dominant direction the camera moves or points toward during the segment. Tag this alongside camera_motion for every segment — the two enums are applied together, not as alternatives.

leftLeft

Camera moves or points toward screen-left. Pairs naturally with pan (rotating left) or truck_left (sliding left).

rightRight

Camera moves or points toward screen-right. Pairs naturally with pan (rotating right) or truck_right (sliding right).

upUp

Camera moves or points upward. Pairs naturally with tilt (rotating up) or an upward pedestal/crane move.

downDown

Camera moves or points downward. Pairs naturally with tilt (rotating down), pedestal_down, or crane_down.

pushPush

Moving toward the subject/scene ("push in"). Pairs with dolly_in (physical push) or zoom_in (lens push).

pullPull

Moving away from the subject/scene ("pull out"). Pairs with dolly_out (physical pull) or zoom_out (lens pull).

noneNone

No dominant directional component — used for static segments, in-place rotations without a clear single sweep direction, or handheld segments where the shake has no consistent directional bias.
